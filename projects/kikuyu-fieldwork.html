Linguistic Background

The Kikuyu (Gĩkũyũ) language (code E51 in Guthrie's classification) belongs to the Bantu branch of the Niger-Congo language family and is typically spoken in central Kenya among Kikuyu tribal members. There are roughly 7 million speakers of Kikuyu. Intergenerational transmission of Kikuyu is strong, although younger generations are innovative in their language use and may not understand older expressions as well. Broad use of Kikuyu online supports the language's vitality among most demographics.

Multilingualism among Kikuyu speakers is common as Swahili and English are the official languages of Kenya and taught in schools. Kikuyu exhibits great regional variation.

Consultant Profile

Our consultant is a male between 40 and 50 years old and was born in Rumuruti, Kenya as a member of the Kikuyu tribe. Kikuyu is his native language, or as he puts it, the language in which he can express himself in a deeper way. He studied it in school from approximately grades one to three. The primary language of instruction in school was English, which he is still proficient in and uses frequently. Swahili was also taught in school and functions as the national language of Kenya and a lingua franca with other tribes. Code switching between Kikuyu, Swahili, and English was common in Kenya. While living in Finland, he has also learned some Finnish for basic interactions.

At home, he still speaks mostly Kikuyu with his wife and children. English is the predominant language at work. He reads and writes English, Swahili, and Kikuyu when communicating online with his friends and family. He mentioned being exposed to the Sheng slang spoken by the younger generation in Kenya but not being able to understand it very well. He suggests that younger speakers of Kikuyu may not understand some traditional vocabulary and idioms either. His grandparents only spoke Kikuyu and never used other languages.

During his time in Kenya, he mostly lived in Rumuruti, but he also lived for three years in Thika. He first moved to Finland in 2004 to study information technology in Oulu where he received a bachelor's degree. He has worked in the IT industry in Finland and has also lived in Turku and now Helsinki, his current residence.

Tense, Aspect, and Mood

Kikuyu verbs are generally constructed by stringing together morphemes using the following paradigm:

Focus – Subject – Negative – Tense – Object – Root – Applicative – Aspect – Final Vowel

The following tables provide the morphemes for tense and aspect, respectively.

Tense Prefix Example Translation
Remote Past ya- ny-au nĩ-ya-teng'era-ga the cat ran (long ago)
Medial Past ra- ny-au nĩ-ra-teng'era-ga ira the cat ran (yesterday)
Recent Past gũ- ny-au nĩ-gũ-teng'era-ga the cat ran (recent, today)
Present Progressive ra- ny-au nĩ-ra-teng'er-a ũmũthi the cat is running today
Near Future gũ- / kũ- ny-au nĩ-gũ-teng'er-a ũmũthi the cat will run today
Remote Future ga- ny-au nĩ-ga-teng'er-a rũciũ the cat will run tomorrow
Table 7. Tense prefixes
Aspect Affix Example Translation
Habitual / Imperfective -ga ny-au nĩ-teng'era-ga the cat often runs
Perfect -te nĩ-ng-ũ-rĩ-te I have eaten (previously)
Table 8. Aspect affixes

Regarding mood, the imperative form of the verb has only the final vowel and no additional morphemes, as in cin-a (burn) and rĩ-a (eat).

Applicatives and Causatives

Applicatives

Applicatives in Kikuyu are a valence-increasing operation marked by the suffix -ĩr. The additional argument is often used to mark the beneficiary of an action. Example (1) shows a sentence without an applicative. Example (2) shows the same sentence with an applicative. Note the -ĩr suffix appears and adds another prefix to describe the benefactor, in this case gũ- for the second-person singular.

(1)
nĩ-ndĩ-ra-thondek-a mũ-gate
FOC-1SG.SUBJ-PRES.PROG-make-FV CL3-bread
'I am making bread.'
(2)
nĩ-ndĩ-ra-gũ-thondek-ĩr-a mũ-gate
FOC-1SG.SUBJ-PRES.PROG-2SG.OBJ-make-APPL-FV CL3-bread
'I am making bread for you.'

Causatives

Causatives in Kikuyu are also valence-increasing, but the additional argument is an agent who makes another participant complete an action. Some causatives are lexical, so the causative meaning is included in the verb root as in example (3) below with the verb he (to give). Other causatives are analytic, meaning they use the modal verb tũma (to send) to convey the causative meaning like in example (4).

(3)
mũ-thuri nĩ-a-ra-he ny-oni i-rio
CL1-man FOC-CL1.SUBJ-PRES.PROG-give CL9-bird CL8-food
'The man is feeding the bird food.'
(4)
mũ-thuri nĩ-a-ra-tũm-a nyoni ĩ-rĩ-e
CL1-man FOC-CL1-PRES.PRG-make-FV CL9.bird CL9-eat-FV
'The man is making the bird eat.'

Existential and Possessive Constructions

Possessive Constructions

Possession in Kikuyu is expressed using the word na (with). Personal suffixes can be applied directly to na to mark ownership as in the table below.

Person Prefix Example Translation
1SG ndĩ- ndĩ-na ny-au I have a cat
2SG wĩ- wĩ-na ny-au you have a cat
3SG e- e-na ny-au she/he has a cat
1PL twĩ- twĩ-na ny-au we have a cat
2PL mwĩ- mwĩ-na ny-au you all have a cat
3PL me- me-na ny-au they have a cat
Table 9. Possessive construction

In a more extended form, the possessive construction uses a copular verb:

(5)
mũ-thuri oima na ny-au
CL1-man RECENT.PST.COP with CL9-cat
'The man had a cat today.'
(6)
ndĩ-ra-rĩ na ny-au
1SG.SUBJ-MED.PST-COP with CL9-cat
'I had a cat yesterday.'

Existential Constructions

Existential and locative expressions are also constructed using the word na (with) and a copular verb. See the examples below:

(7)
kwĩ-na ny-ama ny-ũmba
PRES.COP-with CL9-meat CL9-house
'There is/are meat(s) in the house(s).'
(8)
kwĩ-na mũ-thuri ny-ũmba
PRES.COP-with CL1-man CL9-house
'There is a man in the house.'
(9)
kwa-rĩ na ny-ama ny-ũmba
REMOTE.PST-COP with CL9-meat CL9-house
'There was meat in the house (distal past).'
(10)
kwa-rĩ na mũ-thuri ny-ũmba
REMOTE.PST-COP with CL1-man CL9-house
'There was a man in the house (distal past).'

Questions and Negation

Questions

Some standard question words can be found in the table below. Some question words accept a noun class prefix and just their roots are displayed.

English Kikuyu Example Translation
who nũũ nũũ ũ-ra-rĩ-a? who is eating?

Table 10. Question words

Sometimes Kikuyu makes lexical distinctions using tones. The statement ngui nĩrarĩa has a relatively flat tone throughout while the question form ngui nĩrarĩa? has a high tone on the subject and a falling tone on the verb.

Negation

The negation marker in Kikuyu is the prefix ti- appended to the verb root. The focus particle nĩ- is also removed. The order of the prefix depends on the subject marker. If the subject marker is a single vowel, the negation marker is before the subject marker and morphs into the nd- prefix as in example (11). Otherwise, the negation marker ti- comes after the subject marker like in example (12).

(11)
nd-ũ-rĩ-aga ny-ama
NEG-2SG.SUBJ-eat-HAB CL9-meat
'You do not eat meat.'
(12)
tũ-ti-rĩ-aga ny-ama
1PL.SUBJ-NEG-eat-HAB CL9-meat
'We do not eat meat.'

Beating "6 nimmt!" with Monte Carlo Minimax Search

Published June 14, 2026

The German card game 6 nimmt! (also known as Take 5! or Categories) presents a fascinating challenge for artificial intelligence. With simple rules but highly chaotic interactions, it is a game of imperfect information and simultaneous decisions.

Rules of the Game

The goal of the game is to be the player with the fewest points. A typical deck is numbered from 1 to 104. Everyone is dealt 10 cards, and 4 cards are laid out on the table to start 4 rows. The rest of the deck is set aside.

Each turn, everyone picks a card to play and reveals it at the same time. The cards are played in order from lowest to highest. Cards are placed on top of the row whose top card is lower and closest in value. If the row already contains five cards, the player collects those cards and leaves their card as the start of the new row. If the card is lower than all the top cards, the player must choose a row to collect.

Each card has a number of bullheads. Each bullhead collected is one point. The game continues until everyone is out of cards.

The Search AI Strategy

To beat human heuristics and reinforcement learning models, I developed a Monte Carlo Minimax Search AI. The core algorithm operates as follows:

  1. Monte Carlo Sampling: Because opponent hands are hidden, the AI samples the distribution of cards left in circulation (all cards minus those in rows, in its own hand, or previously played). It shuffles this hidden pool and distributes simulated hands to the opponents.
  2. Minimax Search Look-ahead: For each sample, the game is treated as a perfect-information game. The AI performs a recursive search of a certain depth, simulating player and opponent actions at each step. Opponents are modeled as strategic agents.
  3. Evaluation: The agent computes the expected penalty (bull heads collected) for each card in its hand across all simulations, choosing the card that minimizes its projected penalty.
